Stories

Concerns as data crisis undermining the seriousness of “climate refugees” issue

Lack of data on “climate refugees” and a wide controversy regarding the existing data, mostly based on hypothesis, is undermining the seriousness of this issue, experts say.

Climate-driven movement of people was already under way to the world’s cities long ago, albeit slowly. With climate change hitting the planet faster than scientists originally thought, a great upheaval of climate migration has already started. Whereas low-lying developing countries like Bangladesh are burning already, developed countries like the United States of America and Europe have also started to feel the heat.

Call for recognition of “climate refugees” to address data crisis and future challenges

When two category 4 hurricanes destroyed homes, livelihoods and access to clean water massively in Honduras, Guatemala and El Salvador in November 2020, people poured across the border into Mexico and headed towards the United States. These people got “refugee” status.

But this is not the case for Mahmud and Rashida (names changed), two old men and women in their 70s from Cox’s Bazar area of Bangladesh, who have lost everything including homes and livelihoods to the tropical cyclone in 1991. Not for Abdur Rahim too, a traditional farmer in his 40s from Rangpur area who has lost income from his farming lands due to regular droughts in this northern part of Bangladesh.

7 ways journalists deal with no data

Initiating new approaches to storytelling is essential to keep pace with the audience’s expectations and interests. Doing data journalism is said to be one of the most effective tools in this regard. However journalists sometimes find it hard to get a relevant dataset they want to investigate. In the developing and least developed countries like Bangladesh lack of data is more severe as there are not many government and institutional initiatives to compile data.

We looked at how journalists take different ways to address this data crisis and make some compelling stories on the topic where finding readily available data is almost impossible.

Journalists, researchers chase much on climate crisis, but “forget” the victims

Climate is a widely researched and reported issue but the victims of the climate crisis are not, compared to war and the war victims, data from a research database and a news database reveals.

According to an analysis the word “climate” appeared over 839k times in the headlines of research articles on the Google Scholar database. 2.69% of them included the terms “climate refugees” or “climate migration” anywhere in the articles.

Reporting on “climate refugees” going down as the world lacks interest

Number of reports with the terms “climate refugees” is going down recently, according to a text analysis of a news database.

During 2011 and 2021, at least 516 news headlines in the Nexis news database carried the terms “climate refugees”. The number was highest in 2017, after which it was going down every year.

Analysis: Migration, both internal and external, higher in climate hotspots

The world saw more than 342 million internal disaster displacements during the last 14 years due to climate related events. And the countries with higher rates of disaster displacements are the homes to the big numbers of international migrants, refugees and asylum seekers in other countries, according to an analysis of three international databases.

As no authentic data on “climate refugees” is available, analysing some figures like the above can help get a general idea on the movement of people from their places of origin due to the climate events.

Climate-hit areas getting vacant, big cities packed

As the coastlines are disappearing due to sea advances and lands becoming uninhabitable due to salinity Bhola, a low-lying island district about 300 km south of Dhaka, Bangladesh is gradually emptying out. Big cities including Dhaka and its slums, on the other hand, are continuing to become overcrowded by displaced “climate refugees”.

Among them is Sharif (name changed) who took sanctuary in Dhaka’s ‘Bhola slum’ 25 years ago. Over the years this slum has become a shelter for thousands of others from Bhola, a 6 feet (1.8 m) above ocean level island at the meeting point of two major rivers with the Bay of Bengal.
